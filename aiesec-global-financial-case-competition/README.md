# Finance Case Competition: 3-Year Financial Strategy for AIESEC International

## Overview
This project was a finance case competition focused on developing a **3-year financial sustainability strategy** for **AIESEC International**, a global youth-led organisation operating across more than 100 countries and territories.

The task required evaluating the organisation’s financial performance, identifying structural risks, calculating consolidated financial statements, and proposing a practical strategy that balanced:
- **financial sustainability**
- **organisational growth**
- and **continued accessibility of programmes to students**. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

## Project Context
The case brief positioned the team as **external financial advisors** to AIESEC International. The organisation’s key revenue streams were:
- exchange programme participation fees
- global affiliation fees paid by Member Committees
- and corporate / institutional partnerships. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

The central challenge was that, although AIESEC had grown in membership and exchange volume, it was still facing:
- inconsistent fee collection
- rising operational costs
- and limited financial reserves. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

The final deliverable was a **5–7 slide strategic recommendation presentation** developed under time pressure. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

## My Contribution
My contribution focused on the **financial diagnosis and strategic recommendation side** of the case. This included:
- identifying the organisation’s key financial risks
- analysing the relative sustainability of AIESEC’s different programmes
- assessing regional differences in growth and financial potential
- proposing prioritised interventions to improve revenue reliability
- and contributing to the financial assumptions used for the three-year outlook. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

## Financial Diagnosis

### 1. Thin programme margins
One of the main issues identified in the case was the weak profitability of some exchange programmes. Using the case data:

- **Global Volunteer**  
  average fee = **£500**  
  average cost to deliver = **£490**  
  margin = **£10**

- **Global Talent**  
  average fee = **£570**  
  average cost to deliver = **£500**  
  margin = **£70**

- **Global Teacher**  
  average fee = **£570**  
  average cost to deliver = **£410**  
  margin = **£160**. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

This shows that **Global Volunteer**, despite being the largest programme by volume, had very limited surplus per exchange and therefore provided little room for absorbing shocks or funding reinvestment.

### 2. Unreliable affiliation-fee collection
A major structural weakness in the case was the gap between **expected affiliation revenue** and **actual collected revenue**. For example:

- 2022: expected **£800,000**, collected **£620,000**
- 2023: expected **£887,500**, collected **£710,000**
- 2024: expected **£1,036,000**, collected **£760,000**
- 2025: expected **£1,092,000**, collected **£790,000**. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

This means that a large share of theoretically available revenue was not reliably entering the organisation, creating forecasting risk and weakening liquidity.

### 3. Limited reserve capacity
The brief also states that AIESEC International held reserves equivalent to only **around three months of operating expenses**, which is low for a global organisation with uneven collection patterns and exposure to programme volatility. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

That makes the organisation more vulnerable to short-term revenue shortfalls or cost shocks.

## Programme Sustainability Assessment
A key insight from the case is that financial sustainability differs significantly across programmes.

- **Least financially sustainable:** Global Volunteer  
  It dominates exchange volume but produces the lowest margin per exchange. In 2025, Global Volunteer accounted for **20,200** of **25,530** total exchanges, meaning the majority of AIESEC activity sat in its least profitable programme. 

- **More financially sustainable:** Global Talent and Global Teacher  
  These programmes generate much stronger unit economics and can contribute more effectively to surplus generation. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

This made programme mix an important strategic consideration in the final recommendation.

## Regional Sustainability Assessment
The 2024 regional data showed meaningful variation in exchange performance:

- **Asia Pacific:** 8,930 exchanges, **8%** YoY growth
- **Africa:** 3,470 exchanges, **15%** YoY growth
- **Europe:** 6,760 exchanges, **3%** YoY growth
- **Americas:** 6,370 exchanges, **−2%** YoY growth. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

From a financial-strategy perspective:
- **Asia Pacific** appeared strongest in terms of both scale and healthy growth
- **Africa** showed the strongest growth momentum
- **Americas** appeared least attractive near term due to contraction
- **Europe** looked stable but slower-growing. 

This suggested that future expansion and investment should not be geographically neutral.

## Strategic Recommendations

### 1. Improve revenue reliability through fee reform
The most urgent recommendation was to address under-collection of affiliation fees. My notes proposed **price discrimination / differentiated fee structures**, recognising that the same flat fee may create very different burdens across countries. :contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14}

The logic was:
- lower collection issues in more financially constrained committees
- reduce default pressure
- improve predictability of cash inflows
- while preserving global membership accessibility.

### 2. Rebalance toward higher-margin programmes
A second recommendation was to reduce dependence on **Global Volunteer** as the dominant revenue engine and gradually increase emphasis on **Global Talent** and **Global Teacher**, both of which have stronger margins. 

This was not framed as cutting the volunteer programme sharply, since the case makes clear that exchange programmes are core to AIESEC’s mission and cannot be reduced significantly without harming member experience or organisational reputation. :contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}

Instead, the strategy was to **rebalance growth**, not to dismantle mission-critical activities.

### 3. Build financial reserves
The organisation’s small reserve base meant that part of future surpluses should be retained gradually to improve resilience. My notes explicitly recommended that reserve-building happen over time rather than through a sudden large diversion of funds. :contentReference[oaicite:17]{index=17}

This is a strong recommendation because it improves stability without assuming unrealistic short-term surplus capacity.

### 4. Expand supplementary revenue
Additional ideas included:
- a **donation mechanism** for supporters willing to contribute more
- stronger use of **marketing, partnerships, and endorsements**
- and broader visibility-building to support revenue diversification. :contentReference[oaicite:18]{index=18}

These ideas are especially important because they reduce reliance on one weakly collected revenue stream.

## Financial Projection Logic
My case notes indicate that the three-year projection was built using assumptions such as:
- stable fee and cost levels
- regional exchange growth based on recent trends
- and an average growth rate applied to future exchange activity. :contentReference[oaicite:19]{index=19}

The notes also acknowledge a useful limitation: **uniform growth assumptions are unrealistic**, because financial performance is affected by many local and global factors. That is a good point to include in the portfolio because it shows awareness of modelling limitations rather than overclaiming precision. :contentReference[oaicite:20]{index=20}

## Prioritisation and Implementation
The proposed implementation order in the notes was:

1. **Fee reform / price discrimination first**  
   because it addresses the most immediate problem: unreliable collection. :contentReference[oaicite:21]{index=21}

2. **Donation / supplementary contribution mechanisms next**  
   because they are relatively easy to launch and can provide marginal revenue upside. :contentReference[oaicite:22]{index=22}

3. **Reserve-building gradually over time**  
   through retention of a modest portion of surpluses. :contentReference[oaicite:23]{index=23}

4. **Programme-mix rebalancing and marketing expansion**  
   as longer-horizon initiatives, since they depend more heavily on demand, local capacity, and execution. :contentReference[oaicite:24]{index=24}

This prioritisation is a strong portfolio point because it distinguishes between urgent liquidity fixes and longer-term structural strategy.

## Why This Project Matters
This project is valuable because it demonstrates applied financial reasoning under realistic constraints:
- the organisation has a mission it cannot compromise easily
- its largest programme is not its most profitable
- expected revenue is not the same as collected revenue
- and strategic recommendations must balance finance, accessibility, and growth.

That is much closer to real business decision-making than a purely theoretical finance exercise.

## What I Learned
This project strengthened my understanding of how financial sustainability depends not only on headline growth, but on the reliability and quality of revenue. In particular, it showed that large programme volumes do not automatically translate into strong financial health, and that a good strategy must distinguish between scale, margin, cash collection, and resilience. It also reinforced the importance of sequencing recommendations rather than presenting disconnected ideas.
